05 Ad policies & your rights

This module is general information, not legal advice — SIFT isn't a law firm, and rules vary by country and change over time. But knowing roughly what's already required helps you tell "this ad is just annoying" from "this ad shouldn't legally exist."

Why this is regulated at all

Advertising to children sits under stricter rules than advertising to adults in most of the world, for a fairly simple reason: regulators generally accept that kids can't reliably tell they're being persuaded the way an adult can (Module 04 covers why), which makes ordinary ad tactics disproportionately effective — and disproportionately worth limiting — on them specifically.

United States

COPPA

The Children's Online Privacy Protection Act has governed US children's data since 2000, and it was substantially updated in 2025 — the first major revision since 2013, with full compliance required as of April 2026. The headline change: services can no longer bundle a child's data-sharing consent into general terms of service. Getting a parent's agreement to use the service is no longer enough to also target ads at their kid using that data — that now needs its own, separate, opt-in consent.

COPPA applies to services either directed at children under 13, or that know a specific user is under 13 regardless of the service's general audience.

European Union

The Digital Services Act

The EU's Digital Services Act takes a blunter approach for a broader age range: under Article 28, platforms simply cannot show a minor — anyone under 18 — an ad based on behavioral profiling, once the platform has reasonable certainty the user is a minor. Not "with extra consent," not "with a parent's permission" — not allowed at all. This has applied since February 2024, with the European Commission publishing detailed enforcement guidelines in mid-2025.

The UK has its own, similarly-spirited framework (the Children's Code) built on the same underlying idea: default settings for anyone who might be a child should assume they are one.

The actual gap

Law, policy, and what's actually being served

Here's the gap SIFT exists inside of: a law being on the books, a platform having a written policy against something, and that thing genuinely never reaching a kid's feed are three separate claims. Platforms mostly want to comply — enforcement at the scale of billions of ads is a genuinely hard engineering problem, not just a willpower one, and targeting systems make mistakes even when the policy itself is sound.

This is what "transparency" means in practice

You generally can't audit a platform's ad-serving system yourself. What you can do is see what actually showed up on the screen in front of you — which is exactly what AdSentinel is for, and why AdDashboard exists to aggregate that across many people rather than leaving it as one person's anecdote. Module 06 walks through using both.
