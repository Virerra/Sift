02 Internet safety basics

None of this is SIFT-specific — it's the general foundation everything else in this guide sits on top of. Five habits worth building, none of which require becoming a different kind of parent.

Accounts

Passwords and accounts

The single highest-leverage habit: unique passwords per account, and a password manager doing the remembering instead of a notebook or a reused word-and-number pattern. Most kids' first real security lesson is watching how their parents handle this — it's worth them seeing you use one.

Where a platform offers two-factor authentication, turn it on for anything that matters. It's the difference between a leaked password being an inconvenience and being an actual breach.

Privacy

Personal information and oversharing

Kids generally don't yet have a strong sense of what's meant to stay private — a school uniform in a photo, a house number visible in the background, a location tag on a post are all pieces of a puzzle, not obviously sensitive on their own. Worth talking through concretely: what's in a photo besides the person in it, and why a stranger piecing together "which school, what street" from a few posts is a real thing, not a hypothetical.

A useful frame, not a rule

"Would you be comfortable if a stranger at the park saw this?" works better as an ongoing conversation than a one-time list of banned topics — lists get worked around, habits of thought don't.

Contact

People they only know online

Most kids will end up talking to someone online they've never met in person — a teammate in a game, someone in a fan community. That alone isn't a red flag. What's worth watching for is a pattern: an adult or much-older stranger pushing to move a conversation to a private or less-supervised channel, discouraging a kid from mentioning the friendship to a parent, or escalating quickly from casual chat toward anything that feels secretive.

The goal isn't to name every tactic — it's to make sure a kid already has the instinct that secrecy from a parent, urged by another person, is itself the warning sign, regardless of what that secrecy is about.

Balance

Screen time, without the fight

Blanket time limits tend to produce negotiation, not habits. What tends to work better: naming specific times that are screen-free by default (meals, the hour before bed) rather than a daily minute budget to track and dispute. Sleep is the one that matters most physiologically — a screen in the bedroom overnight is worth being deliberate about regardless of the exact minute count during the day.

The habit that matters most

Making "come to me" actually true

Every guideline above works better in the presence of one thing: a kid who believes that if something online goes wrong — a scary message, a mistake, something they clicked they shouldn't have — telling you makes it better, not worse. That belief isn't built by a single conversation; it's built by how the last time they told you something went.

If the honest reaction to a kid's confession was ever anger or punishment, that's worth repairing directly — "I overreacted last time, and I want you to know you can still tell me anything" is a real, effective sentence.
