04 How advertising targets kids

This is the module SIFT exists because of. Five mechanisms worth understanding — not to make you suspicious of every ad your kid sees, but to make the genuinely bad ones easier to spot.

Mechanism

Dark patterns: language built to skip the decision

"Only 2 left," "you've been selected," "claim your free prize" — these phrases aren't accidental. They're built to trigger a fast, emotional reaction instead of the slower "do I actually want this" one. Adults have usually built up enough exposure to feel the manipulation; kids often haven't yet, which is exactly why this category of ad disproportionately targets them.

This is literally the first thing AdSentinel checks for — see Module 06 for what that actually looks like in practice.

Mechanism

Why this specific ad, on this specific video

Ads aren't randomly assigned — platforms infer age, interests, and likely purchasing influence from watch history, engagement patterns, and account signals, then serve accordingly. A gambling or dating ad showing up against cartoon content isn't necessarily a rule someone broke on purpose; it's often a targeting system making an inference that happened to be wrong, or a category the platform simply hasn't restricted as tightly as you'd expect.

Either way, the mechanism producing it is worth understanding: it's not usually a human choosing to show your kid that specific ad, it's an automated system optimizing for engagement with imperfect guardrails.

Mechanism

Age-restricted categories, showing up anyway

Gambling, dating apps, alcohol and vaping products, "get rich quick" crypto schemes — all restricted in most places for real reasons, and all things AdSentinel specifically watches for. When one of these appears against content clearly made for kids, that gap between the platform's stated policy and what's actually being served is exactly the kind of pattern worth flagging — both to the platform and, if you're using AdSentinel, to AdDashboard's aggregate public statistics.

Mechanism

When an ad doesn't look like an ad

Sponsored content, "gifted" products, and creator endorsements blur a line that used to be sharper: a TV commercial announced itself, but a favorite creator casually using a product on camera often doesn't feel like advertising at all — to a kid or, honestly, to plenty of adults. Most platforms require some form of paid-partnership disclosure, but it's frequently small, brief, or easy to miss entirely.

Worth asking directly, sometimes: "do you think they got paid to say that?" It builds the habit of asking the question at all, which matters more than any single right answer.

Why this works

Why kids specifically

This isn't about kids being less intelligent — it's developmental. The ability to reliably recognize "this is trying to persuade me" as distinct from "this is just information" develops gradually through childhood, and even when a kid can state the concept, the instinct to apply it in the moment, mid-video, lags behind. That gap between knowing and doing is precisely what fast-moving, emotionally-charged ad tactics are built to exploit.

A concrete red flag, always worth acting on

Anything that sexualizes a person, character, or mascot has no place anywhere near content made for kids, regardless of what product it's selling. You don't need to categorize exactly why it feels wrong before reporting it — that instinct alone is enough. Module 07 covers exactly where that report goes.
