What SIFT does

SIFT is a small set of open-source tools that help you see what ads are actually being shown to kids on YouTube, Facebook, Instagram, and TikTok — and give you a way to do something about it. AdSentinel is a browser extension that scans the ads on a page you're looking at and flags the ones that match known manipulative or age-inappropriate patterns, with a plain reason for every flag. It runs entirely in your own browser — nothing is sent anywhere unless you choose to share it.

This page is the plain-language companion to that tool: what AdSentinel can and can't catch, what to watch for yourself, and where to actually send a report when something's wrong.

What to look for

You don't need special tools to catch most of this — it's mostly about trusting a feeling of "that's off" and knowing where to send it. A few concrete patterns worth knowing:

Fake urgency and fake prizes

"Only 2 left," "you've been selected," "claim your free prize" — language designed to short-circuit a considered decision, aimed at anyone, but especially effective on kids who haven't built up the same skepticism yet.

Age-restricted products on kid content

Gambling, dating apps, alcohol or vaping products, "get rich quick" crypto schemes — these are restricted in most places for good reason, and seeing them served against cartoons or kids' gaming content is a real signal something in the platform's ad targeting broke down, not a one-off fluke.

Anything that just feels wrong

Sometimes it's the framing, the imagery, the pacing — something that doesn't sit right even if you can't immediately name why. That instinct is worth acting on. You don't need to be able to categorize why an ad feels predatory or inappropriate before reporting it — describing what you saw and where is enough. Trust it and report it; let the platform's own reviewers make the final call.

How AdSentinel's flags work — and where they stop

Worth understanding clearly, because it changes what a "clean" scan actually means: AdSentinel reads text — an ad's copy, its accessible labels, which network served it. It does not and currently cannot look at an ad's actual image or video content. A scan with zero flags means nothing text-based tripped a rule; it is not a guarantee the ad is fine. If an ad's images or video feel off even though the text passed clean, that's exactly the gap between what a machine can check and what a person watching can see — report it anyway.

How to report a bad ad

Two places a report actually helps — both are worth doing, they serve different purposes:

Report it to the platform directly

This is the report that can get a specific ad taken down. Every major platform has a built-in reporting flow, usually behind a small menu (often three dots, "···" or similar) on the ad itself.

  • YouTube: the "···" menu on the ad, or Google's ad complaint form
  • Facebook / Instagram: the "···" menu on the ad → Report ad
  • TikTok: tap and hold the ad, or use the flag icon on it

Report it to SIFT

This doesn't take the ad down, but it's what turns one person's "this seemed off" into a pattern others can see and act on. If you have AdSentinel installed, use its Export button, then open an issue using the ad report template and attach it. No account needed on your end beyond a GitHub login to open the issue itself.
